# Style anchor — Minh Ha-Duong's pre-AI English academic prose

Prepared for ticket 0173. Purpose: anchor a machine translation of the French
manuscript (v2.0.5 base rebuild, ticket 0172) in the author's own English voice.

## Author decisions (2026-07-08)

- **Selection**: Nature 1997 removed (too specific), Kluwer 1998 chapter
  removed (too old). Added on author request: the IPCC-as-scientific-corpus
  chapter (`Ha-Duong2018:commentaires`) as a **register anchor** — it is in
  French, so it anchors argumentative posture, not English idiom.
- **Language policy — choice (a)**: preserve the author's style traits
  (measured hedging, concrete analogies, question-closings, light irony) but
  produce grammatically correct **British English**. Do not reproduce
  non-native slips; do not over-polish into flourish either.

## Selection criteria

- First-author (or clearly author-voiced) English journal articles or chapters,
  1997–2011 — safely pre-LLM.
- Prose-rich venues over equation-heavy ones: Climatic Change, Global
  Environmental Change, Nature, S.A.P.I.EN.S, Integrated Assessment.
- Passages chosen from introductions, discussions, conclusions: no equations,
  no lists, no captions. Capped at ~120 words each, at most two per article.
- All full texts found locally under `/home/haduong/CNRS/html/files/` (the
  author's website sources). No web fetching was needed.

## Voice traits observed across the corpus

- Opens with a plain question or a blunt reframing ("Is CCS a valuable option
  even if the CO2 does not stay underground?").
- Concrete analogies for abstract economics (leaky storage as a bank loan;
  inertia as Janus).
- Modest, measured hedging: "we suggest", "in our view", "it might not be a bad
  idea" — one hedge at a time, often with dry understatement.
- Short declarative closings that land a point rather than recapitulate.
- Willing first-person singular and plural; occasional light irony
  ("a touch of sarcasm").
- Non-native but confident syntax in the originals — per the author's
  decision (a) above, the translation corrects the grammar (British English)
  while keeping the rhythm, hedging and analogy habits.

## 1. Ha-Duong & Loisel (2009), Climatic Change — editorial comment

**Bib key:** `Ha-Duong.ea2009:zero`
**Reference:** Ha-Duong M., Loisel R. (2009). Zero is the only acceptable
leakage rate for geologically stored CO2: an editorial comment. *Climatic
Change* 93(3):311–317. doi:10.1007/s10584-009-9560-z
**Why selected:** Pure argumentative prose, no equations; the closest genre to
the manuscript (economist arguing about social construction of a policy
quantity). First author.
**Accessibility:** Full text local
(`/home/haduong/CNRS/html/files/HaDuong.ea-2008-ZeroIsTheOnlyAcceptableLeakageRate.pdf`).

**Passage 1 (introduction, opening move):**

> From an economic point of view the analysis of CCS with leakage is
> essentially a question of intertemporal valuation. With leakage, storing
> carbon in the ground is like taking a loan at the bank. One gets a benefit
> today. Then one pays back in a sequence of small installments. Payments sum
> up to more than the borrowed amount.

**Passage 2 (closing):**

> From economic perspective, when CCS with leakage is assimilated to a carbon
> loan, accepting leaking systems is like taking increasing amounts of
> financial debt. Recent macroeconomic developments remind that this is not
> sustainable.

## 2. Ha-Duong, Swart, Bernstein & Petersen (2007), Global Environmental Change

**Bib key:** `Ha-Duong.ea2007:uncertainty`
**Reference:** Ha-Duong M., Swart R., Bernstein L., Petersen A. (2007).
Uncertainty management in the IPCC: agreeing to disagree. *Global
Environmental Change* 17(1):8–11. doi:10.1016/j.gloenvcha.2006.12.003
**Why selected:** Editorial on epistemology and institutions — the register
closest to the HET manuscript (how a scientific community negotiates
categories). First author. Dense, discursive prose throughout.
**Accessibility:** Full text local
(`.../files/HaDuong.ea-2007-UncertaintyManagementInTheIPCCAgreeingToDisagree.pdf`).

**Passage 1 (thesis statement):**

> Looking back over three and a half Assessment Reports, we see that the
> Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) has given increasing
> attention to the management and reporting of uncertainties, but coordination
> across working groups (WGs) has remained an issue. We argue that there are
> good reasons for working groups to use different methods to assess
> uncertainty, thus it is better that working groups agree to disagree rather
> than seek to bring everybody on one party line.

**Passage 2 (argumentative turn):**

> While these differences across WGs may be confusing to readers, they are in
> fact both legitimate and appropriate. A one-size-fits-all approach would
> obscure important differences in the type of uncertainties and in the
> methods. The questions which are addressed by the three WGs differ, and
> hence also the approach towards uncertainty should differ.

## 3. Ha-Duong (2009), S.A.P.I.EN.S — "What is the Price of Carbon?"

**Bib key:** `Ha-Duong2009:price`
**Reference:** Ha-Duong M. (2009). What is the Price of Carbon? Five
definitions. *Survey and Perspectives Integrating Environment & Society*
2(1). https://journals.openedition.org/sapiens/793
**Why selected:** Single-author disambiguation essay on the meanings of an
economic quantity — thematically the direct ancestor of the climate-finance
manuscript. Accessible register, light irony, zero equations.
**Accessibility:** Full text local
(`.../files/HaDuong-2009-WhatIsThePriceOfCarbon.pdf`).

**Passage 1 (introduction, ironic register):**

> These prices are in year 2000 US dollars. The scope of this range—from 5 to
> 80—gives reasonable cause for scepticism, or even a touch of sarcasm
> regarding the claims of models that they can provide insight into a future
> fraught with controversy. In fact, since information in this domain is in a
> state of considerable confusion, we feel that it would be more informative
> to clarify what is meant exactly by cost, price or value of carbon.

**Passage 2 (argument on the social meaning of a number):**

> Even if it cannot be measured in the same way, as for instance, sea levels,
> stating that carbon does have a strictly positive social value is, in
> itself, an important step forward. It means that there is agreement on the
> fact that climate change is a real problem and that greenhouse gas emissions
> must be curbed.

## 4. Ha-Duong, Mégie & Hauglustaine (2003), Global Environmental Change

**Bib key:** `Ha-Duong.ea2003:pro-active`
**Reference:** Ha-Duong M., Mégie G., Hauglustaine D. (2003). A pro-active
stratospheric ozone protection scenario. *Global Environmental Change*
13(1):43–49. doi:10.1016/S0959-3780(02)00087-0
**Why selected:** Counterfactual-history argument in policy-analysis prose;
strong narrative openings and closings; first author. Shows the author's
question-as-conclusion device.
**Accessibility:** Full text local
(`.../files/HaDuong.ea-2003-ProActiveStratosphericOzoneScenario.pdf`).

**Passage 1 (introduction, contrarian reframing):**

> The worldwide control on substances depleting the stratospheric ozone layer
> is often regarded as an exemplary success of global environmental policy. We
> would like to qualify this point of view, by observing that while scientific
> warning came as early as the mid seventies, corrective action came too late
> to prevent the infamous 'hole' over the antarctic. It is somewhat disturbing
> to think that the best global atmospheric environmental policy has been
> implemented only after a large-scale non-linearity was observed, in a
> reactive mode.

**Passage 2 (conclusion, understatement close):**

> Yet when there is not much information, under deep uncertainty the different
> courses of action can not always be compared by balancing the costs and the
> benefits. The early action scenario could also have been a rational choice.
> Is not this the essence of the precautionary principle? Our conclusion is
> that the Montreal Protocol was only a partially successful application of
> precaution. It might not be a bad idea to regulate global atmospheric
> environmental issues before surprising non-linearities occur.

## 5. Ha-Duong (2001), Integrated Assessment

**Bib key:** `Ha-Duong2001:transparency`
**Reference:** Ha-Duong M. (2001). Transparency and control in engineering
integrated assessment models. *Integrated Assessment* 2(4):209–218.
doi:10.1023/A:1013384932008
**Why selected:** Single-author essay on scientific practice and institutions;
reflective register with first-person singular; almost entirely prose. A
precursor of the author's reproducibility concerns in the current project.
**Accessibility:** Full text local
(`.../files/HaDuong-2001-TransparencyControlIntegratedAssessment.pdf`).

**Passage 1 (diagnosis):**

> One problem with integrated assessment is that referees are usually not
> asked to verify the model-based results of submitted manuscripts, not even
> audit the source code. They only assess papers against existing literature,
> their intuition and understanding of the field. This reflects the judgment
> that, while the costs of formally reproducing the results are presumed
> large, the additional benefits are presumed small.

**Passage 2 (first-person conviction):**

> Potential transparency remains, since anybody can get the model simply by
> asking. However, I find no reason to be satisfied with potential
> transparency when models source code can be published at low cost with
> today's information technology.

## 6. Ha-Duong, Casman & Morgan (2004), Risk Analysis

**Bib key:** `Ha-Duong.ea2004:bounding`
**Reference:** Ha-Duong M., Casman E.A., Morgan M.G. (2004). Bounding poorly
characterized risks: a Lung Cancer Example. *Risk Analysis* 24(5):1071–1083.
doi:10.1111/j.0272-4332.2004.00508.x
**Why selected:** Methods paper, but the introduction and conclusion carry
clear expository prose about quantification under uncertainty — the "reality
check" framing echoes the manuscript's concern with what numbers can claim.
First author. Middle sections are mathematical; use only intro/conclusion.
**Accessibility:** Full text local
(`.../files/HaDuong.ea-2003-BoundingAnalysis.pdf`, preprint).

**Passage 1 (introduction, problem statement):**

> The familiar "front-to-back" procedure for calculating disease or mortality
> risk from exposure to environmental contaminants ... works best when the
> relevant science is well developed. However, when there are several risk
> factors and the uncertainty about some of them is large, such a procedure
> can lead to estimates for the numbers of cases attributable to the various
> factors that, summed, exceed the total number of cases actually observed.

**Passage 2 (conclusion, plain utility claim):**

> In this paper we have applied the Smets' Transferable Belief Model to
> estimate an upper bound on the fraction of lung cancers caused annually by
> the group of causes for which comprehensive longitudinal studies are
> lacking. Such a result is interesting from a risk management perspective.
> For example it might be used to infer the level of control effort these
> pollutants deserve.
