## Sample 1 — argument register (Introduction, @sec-intro)

**Location:** l. 70, "Two operations run through this history..."

**BEFORE:**

> Two operations run through this history. Commensuration makes heterogeneous
> flows comparable on a common metric -- a grant-equivalent percentage, a
> marker coefficient, a dollar total [@espeland_stevens1998]. Economisation is
> the further step by which the resulting quantity becomes an economic object
> in its own right: given a perimeter and a target, made to grow, and governed
> by the reasoning of leverage and mobilisation [@caliskan_callon2009]. The
> same device often performs both, the second move riding on the first. That
> passage, traced period by period, is the thread running through the rest of
> this article.

**AFTER:**

> Two operations run through this history. The first is commensuration:
> putting unlike flows on one scale, a grant-equivalent percentage, a marker
> coefficient, a dollar total [@espeland_stevens1998]. The second is
> economisation: the quantity so produced becomes an economic object in its
> own right, with a perimeter, a target, and a doctrine of leverage and
> mobilisation to make it grow [@caliskan_callon2009]. One device often does
> both jobs, the second riding on the first. The rest of this article follows
> that passage, period by period.

**What changed and why:** definition-first cadence replaced by two parallel
"The first is / The second is" moves (the anchors introduce abstractions by
enumeration, not by textbook definition); the sentence dash dropped for a
colon; the closing sentence shortened so the paragraph lands on the plan, in
the stressed final slot. Both citation keys unchanged and in place.

## Sample 2 — historical-narrative register (Crystallization, @sec-crystallization)

**Location:** l. 134, "A parallel infrastructure emerged within the UNFCCC
process..."

**BEFORE:**

> A parallel infrastructure emerged within the UNFCCC process. The Standing
> Committee on Finance, established at COP16 in Cancún (2010), was tasked with
> producing biennial assessments of climate finance flows. Where the DAC
> system was designed by and for donors, reporting what they had *provided*,
> the UNFCCC process was also accountable to recipient countries, which
> reported what they had *received*, and the two figures rarely agreed. The
> first Biennial Assessment (2014) estimated total flows for 2011--2012 at
> $340--650 billion per year, and allowed they might be higher still -- a
> range so wide that it illustrated the problem more than it resolved it
> [@unfccc2014biennial]. Two measurement regimes, each embedded in a different
> political constituency, were producing different numbers from the same
> underlying reality. In this battle for the operational definition, the
> OECD's figures became the *de facto* reference for assessing progress
> towards the $100 billion target, because the DAC produced data that was more
> standardised and more timely. The development-economics categories embedded
> in the DAC system won the definitional contest through infrastructural
> incumbency, not intellectual superiority.

**AFTER:**

> A parallel infrastructure emerged within the UNFCCC process. The Standing
> Committee on Finance, established at COP16 in Cancún (2010), was tasked with
> producing biennial assessments of climate finance flows. The DAC system was
> built by and for donors: it counted what they had *provided*. The UNFCCC
> process also answered to recipient countries, which counted what they had
> *received*. The two figures rarely agreed. The first Biennial Assessment
> (2014) put total flows for 2011--2012 at $340--650 billion per year, and
> allowed they might be higher still; a range that wide illustrated the
> problem more than it resolved it [@unfccc2014biennial]. Two measurement
> regimes, each answering to its own constituency, produced different numbers
> from the same reality. The OECD's figures became the *de facto* reference
> for progress towards the $100 billion target, because the DAC delivered data
> that was more standardised and more timely. The development-economics
> categories embedded in the DAC system won the definitional contest the way
> incumbents usually do: the machinery was already running.

**What changed and why:** the 44-word third sentence split into three short
declaratives ending on the blunt "The two figures rarely agreed." (the
anchors' short-sentence rhythm); the sentence dash before "a range so wide"
replaced by a semicolon, so the paragraph's em-dash count falls by one; the
closing "through X, not Y" antithesis recast as a dry positive close, the
anchors' understatement device ("Recent macroeconomic developments remind that
this is not sustainable"). All figures, dates, italics and the citation key
unchanged.

## Sample 3 — appendix-methods register (Appendix A.4)

**Location:** l. 280, "The periodization -- 1990--2006, 2007--2014,
2015--2024 -- rests first on institutional history..."

**BEFORE:**

> The periodization -- 1990--2006, 2007--2014, 2015--2024 -- rests first on
> institutional history: before Bali and Copenhagen the category is not yet
> settled; between 2007 and 2014 the counting devices crystallise; after Paris
> the controversies unfold within an established frame. To test it without
> whispering the answer, the break years are sought while ignoring the
> calendar of the COPs. For each candidate year between 2005 and 2023 we
> compare the thematic composition of the years just before and just after,
> along two complementary measures: the Jensen--Shannon divergence between the
> theme-share vectors, which catches redistribution across themes, and the
> cosine distance between mean embeddings, which catches drift in the
> continuous space of meaning. Each year's score is expressed as a z-score, so
> that the two measures share one scale, and the comparison is run at three
> window widths of two, three and four years.

**AFTER:**

> The periodization -- 1990--2006, 2007--2014, 2015--2024 -- rests first on
> institutional history: before Bali and Copenhagen the category is not yet
> settled; between 2007 and 2014 the counting devices crystallise; after Paris
> the controversies unfold within an established frame. The test must not
> whisper the answer, so we search for the break years while ignoring the
> calendar of the COPs. For each candidate year between 2005 and 2023 we
> compare the thematic composition of the years just before and just after.
> Two measures complement each other: the Jensen--Shannon divergence between
> theme-share vectors catches redistribution across themes; the cosine
> distance between mean embeddings catches drift in the continuous space of
> meaning. Each year's score is expressed as a z-score, so the two measures
> share one scale, and the comparison is run at three window widths of two,
> three and four years.

**What changed and why:** the passive "the break years are sought" given an
agent ("we search" — "we" pending D1); the 55-word measures sentence split so
each measure gets its own clause, one idea per sentence, as in the 2004
bounding paper's methods prose. The opening sentence, with its paired dashes
and the periodization statement, is untouched: the periodization claims and
the em-dash count must not move. All years, window widths and measure names
unchanged.
